Why is it so hard to lose weight with PCOS?

You are eating less. You are moving more. You are doing everything the internet told you to do. And the scale still will not move.

If that is you, please hear this first. You are not lazy, and you are not failing. Your body is playing by different rules, and nobody ever explained them to you.

It was never about willpower

For years you were probably told the same thing. Eat less, move more. When it did not work, the quiet message underneath was that you were not trying hard enough. That message is wrong.

PCOS changes how your body stores fat, handles sugar and responds to a calorie deficit. You were following a plan that was never built for a body like yours. It was the wrong approach, not a lack of discipline.

What PCOS is actually doing

A few things tend to work against women with PCOS at the same time. Not every woman has all of them, and how strongly each one shows up depends on your hormonal type. But these are the usual suspects.

Insulin resistance

With PCOS, your cells often respond less to insulin, the hormone that manages your blood sugar. So your body makes more of it. High insulin tells your body to store fat, especially around the middle, and makes it harder to burn. Eating even less does not fix that signal. Sometimes it makes the cravings worse.

A slower metabolism

Many women with PCOS also deal with thyroid issues or a generally slower metabolic rate. That means the same food and the same workout give you a smaller result than they give someone without it. It is not in your head. The maths really is different for you.

The cravings and energy cycle

High insulin leads to blood sugar crashes. Crashes lead to cravings and low energy. Low energy leads to less movement and more comfort eating. Left alone, the loop tightens month after month. This is not weakness. It is chemistry.

So what actually helps?

Here is the good news. Your body is not broken. It is running on different instructions, and once you work with those instructions instead of against them, things start to move. A few honest starting points:

  • Anchor your meals with protein. It steadies your blood sugar and quiets the cravings more than any diet rule.
  • Do not starve. Crash diets spike the exact hormones you are trying to calm.
  • Prioritise sleep and stress. They drive your hormones as much as food does, and they are the part almost every plan ignores.
  • Be consistent, not perfect. Small, boring, repeatable habits beat a strict plan you cannot hold.

None of this is about doing more. It is about doing the right things for your body, in the right order. That usually means fixing the hormones first. The fat loss follows.
